Interactive Media — A1 Research Project

Luni Interactive Experience

This webpage presents my research responses based on my interaction with the Luni website.

1. What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

The first thing that captured my attention when I opened the homepage was the immersive 3D environment combined with a dreamlike colour palette. The scene presents a stylised miniature world of furniture and architecture, which immediately feels playful and imaginative. The large title “Luniverse” stands out clearly, while the four sections — Omada, Studio, Career, and Luni Life — are positioned below, acting as entry points. The instruction “scroll to explore the luniverse” subtly guided my behaviour and encouraged me to begin interacting with the experience.

2. Spend two minutes with the experience and create a list of each of your discrete actions.

When I first opened the Home page, I was immediately drawn into a visually immersive environment. A 3D miniature world appeared in front of me, surrounded by soft, dreamlike colours. The large title “Luniverse” dominated the screen, giving me the impression that I was entering a digital universe rather than a traditional website.

Below the title, I noticed four main sections: Omada, Studio, Career, and Luni Life. A small line of text reading “scroll to explore the luniverse” encouraged me to begin interacting, so I started scrolling down to explore.

I first entered the Omada section, which introduced itself as “a new experience for sport fans.” The environment was divided into three main areas: a racing track, a basketball court, and a football field. While the racing track appeared purely visual, I discovered interactive elements in the sports sections. When I clicked on the basketball area, I was able to shoot the ball into the hoop, turning the experience into a small game. Similarly, in the football section, I could kick the ball towards the goal. A “potential win” button allowed me to adjust numbers, simulating a betting-like system. When I successfully scored, coins burst onto the screen, creating a rewarding and playful feedback moment. This made the interaction feel engaging and game-like rather than informational.

Next, I moved into the Studio section. The tagline “Endless creativity, endless possibilities, endless ambition, no limits” set the tone for a highly imaginative space. The environment resembled a creative studio or personal lab. I observed multiple detailed elements: a small ecological garden with butterflies, a slide with continuously falling spheres, a cosy seating area with books and a lamp, and a workspace alongside a relaxing coffee corner. The atmosphere felt warm and inspiring. I also discovered subtle interactions—when I clicked on the phone and computer screens, they would turn off and on again. Although simple, these interactions reinforced the idea of digital experimentation and creative play.

I then explored the Luni Life section, which felt more content-rich and documentary in nature. Instead of a fully interactive environment, this section presented real-life moments through images and short descriptions. I browsed through different stories such as team activities, office spaces, and projects like the “Luni Passport.” The horizontal gallery allowed me to scroll through multiple snapshots of daily life, giving me a sense of the company’s culture. Compared to previous sections, this part felt more grounded and authentic, showing how the brand’s values exist in reality.

Finally, I entered the Career section, which resembled a hybrid space between a workplace and a lifestyle environment. I noticed a wide range of objects: a punching bag, a desk, plants, books, a wine glass, a yoga ball, a sofa, a game controller, dog food and toys, and even a frying pan. These elements suggested a balance between work, leisure, and personal life. I discovered two interactive features here. First, when I clicked on the punching bag, it swung from side to side, simulating physical movement. Second, clicking on the frying pan triggered an animation of cooking eggs, which felt surprisingly playful and relatable. These interactions added a strong sense of everyday life and human touch to the experience.

Throughout the entire journey, I remained aware that I could navigate between sections at any time using the menu, but I chose to follow the narrative flow from top to bottom. Overall, the experience felt less like browsing a website and more like exploring an interactive world, where each section revealed a different aspect of the brand.

3. What part of the experience did you spend the most time engaging with?

The section where I spent the most time was the Omada experience. This was mainly because it offered the highest level of interactivity compared to the other sections. While the Home, Studio, Career, and Luni Life sections were visually engaging and exploratory, Omada allowed me to actively participate rather than just observe.

In particular, I found myself repeatedly interacting with the basketball and football mini-games. The ability to shoot and score goals created a sense of challenge and reward. The visual feedback—such as coins bursting out when scoring—made the experience feel satisfying and game-like. Additionally, the “potential win” feature introduced a dynamic element, encouraging me to adjust values and experiment with outcomes.

This interactive loop of action and feedback kept me engaged for a longer period of time. Unlike passive browsing, I was motivated to continue playing to achieve better results, which naturally extended my time in this section.

In contrast, although the Studio and Career sections also included interactive elements, their interactions were more subtle and limited. The Luni Life section focused more on browsing content rather than interaction. As a result, these sections felt more observational, whereas Omada felt immersive and participatory.

Overall, I spent the most time on Omada because it transformed the experience from simply viewing a website into actively engaging with it, making it more memorable and enjoyable.

4. What was the most common action in your two minute interaction with the experience?

The most common action during my interaction was clicking. I repeatedly clicked on different elements to test whether they were interactive, such as the basketball and football in Omada, the devices in Studio, and objects like the punching bag and frying pan in Career.

This behaviour was driven by curiosity, as the website encourages users to explore and discover interactions rather than clearly indicating them. While scrolling was also frequent, clicking played a more active role because it triggered immediate visual feedback and made the experience more engaging.

5. What is your impression of the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

The primary goal of the experience is to communicate Luni’s identity as a creative and playful digital studio. Rather than focusing on delivering information, the website aims to immerse users in an interactive world that reflects its design philosophy, culture, and products.

6. How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

The experience communicates this goal through immersive visuals, minimal text, and interactive elements. Each section represents a different aspect of the brand—Omada focuses on playful interaction, Studio highlights creativity, while Career and Luni Life present culture and everyday experiences.

The use of large typography, simple navigation, and responsive feedback encourages users to explore actively, allowing them to understand the brand through interaction rather than direct explanation.

7. What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time?

My impression is that the experience is designed for short but repeated interactions over time, rather than one long session. The website does not feel like something to read once from beginning to end. Instead, it feels more like a digital environment that invites users to return, explore different sections, and discover new details gradually. Because each section offers a different mood and type of interaction, the experience seems intended to be revisited multiple times.

8. How does the interactive experience communicate how it should be interacted with over time?

The experience communicates this through its open structure and non-linear navigation. There is no strict order, no single task to complete, and no obvious ending point. Instead, users can move between sections freely and test different interactive elements at their own pace. The hidden or subtle interactions also suggest that not everything needs to be understood immediately, which encourages return visits and further exploration over time.

9. What other media forms does the experience reference?

The experience references several other media forms, especially video games, virtual worlds, and interactive installations. Some sections also resemble social media or app interfaces, particularly Omada, which feels like a product demo combined with a mini-game. At the same time, sections such as Luni Life and Career include elements that feel closer to editorial design, lifestyle branding, and digital storytelling.

10. What do these references communicate to you about how you should act when engaging with your research experience?

These references suggest that I should act in a playful, curious, and experimental way. The game-like qualities encourage me to click, test, and explore rather than passively read. The more installation-like and world-building elements also suggest that I should take time to observe the space and notice details. Overall, the experience encourages active participation instead of straightforward consumption.

11. What do these references communicate to you about how you should feel when engaging with your research experience?

They communicate that I should feel curious, playful, and open-minded. The bright visuals, animated interactions, and imaginative 3D environments create a light and engaging mood. At the same time, the more documentary and lifestyle-based sections, such as Luni Life, create a sense of warmth and authenticity. Together, these references make the experience feel both exciting and approachable.

12. What is the most frustrating part of the interaction to you and what makes that part frustrating?

The most frustrating part of the interaction is that some interactive elements are not clearly signposted. In several sections, I had to guess what was clickable and what was only decorative. This made the experience slightly inconsistent, because sometimes my curiosity was rewarded, but other times nothing happened. While this ambiguity supports exploration, it can also make the interaction feel uncertain and slightly less intuitive.

13. What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

The most satisfying part of the interaction is the immediate feedback after performing an action. In Omada, for example, scoring a goal or triggering a successful interaction creates a clear visual reward, which feels exciting and responsive. I also found the smaller interactions in Studio and Career satisfying because they made the digital world feel alive. What makes these moments satisfying is that the website responds directly to my actions, which creates a strong sense of connection between the user and the interface.
